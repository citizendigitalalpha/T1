What to do in an interview

An interview is a discussion in person, by phone or online, between you and and a potential employer. The employer will want to see if you're the right person for the job, and it'll give you the chance to see if the job's one you want.

It's your chance to make a good impression, and show the employer what you have to offer.

Make sure you're prepared. Research the role and organisation, and prepare evidence and examples of your skills and experience.

  • Before the interview
  • During the interview
  • When the employer contacts you after the interview

1. Before the interview

  • find out what kind of interview it'll be so you can prepare - it could be a panel interview, a work-based task, or a group discussion
  • look again at the job description to see what sort of person they're looking for, and think of examples you could give to show you'd be a good fit
  • think about which areas of your CV and application the interviewer might ask you to talk more about, and how you can relate them to the role
  • prepare to talk about common subjects, like why you want the job, your strengths andweaknesses, and your relevant work and life experience
  • prepare some questions about the role and the company to ask at the end of theinterview, but don't ask about pay yet
  • plan what you're going to wear - you'll need to dress smartly and appropriately for the job
  • check how to get to the interview venue, and how long it'll take
  • make sure you leave plenty of time to get there and arrive a little early

2. During the interview

  • make sure your phone's off
  • look alert and attentive, speak clearly and confidently, and don't swear or use slang
  • don't wear very glamorous clothes or strong perfume or aftershave
  • give full answers, don't just say 'yes' or 'no'
  • give examples of when you've used certain skills
  • when providing examples of your experience, describe the situation, the task, the action you took and the result of your action
  • make sure you fully understand the questions you're asked, and ask for moreexplanation if you need to
  • take your time thinking of your answer - it's fine to say you need a moment to think
  • be positive about your experiences, avoid negativity about yourself or any previous roles you've had
  • don't lie – the interviewer may see through you and, even if you get the job, youremployer can dismiss you if they find out you've been dishonest
  • don't let your nerves show too much – use breathing techniques and try to remember a few nerves are normal
  • don't be arrogant and assume you've got the job – nothing turns off employers morethan someone who is disrespectful and over-confident
  • don't discuss controversial topics like religion, politics or gender relations

3. When the employer contacts you after the interview

  • ask for feedback on your performance - if you weren't successful think about how youcould use their comments to improve for next time
  • if you're offered a job and decide you don't want it, thank the employer politely, as you may want to work for them in future
