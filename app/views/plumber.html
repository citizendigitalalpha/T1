Plumber Heating and ventilation engineer, HVAC engineer

Plumbers fit and service heating systems. They also repair faults in hot and cold water supplies and drainage networks.

Salary: £18,000 to £35,000 average per year
Hours: 37 to 40 hours per week

1. Entry requirements

To become a plumber, you'll do an industry-recognised qualification eg a Level 3 (NVQ) Diploma in Domestic Plumbing and Heating. The usual way to do this is through an apprenticeship.

You could also take a foundation course in plumbing skills at college. This may help you to find a trainee position with a company. Once working, you can do further training.

There are short courses in plumbing offering useful background knowledge, but won't qualify you as a plumber. Summitskills and Go Construct have more information about careers and qualifications in plumbing.

2. Skills required

You'll need:

  • the ability to follow technical drawings and plans
  • practical skills
  • customer care skills
  • the ability to follow safe working practices

3. What you'll do

You'll service and repair heating, water and sanitation systems in domestic and commercial premises.

You'll be:

  • installing water, drainage and heating systems
  • finding and fixing faults
  • servicing gas and oil-fired central heating systems and radiators
  • installing and fixing domestic appliances like showers, cookers, gas fires and washing machines
  • servicing air-conditioning and ventilation units
  • dealing with emergency call outs like boiler breakdowns or blocked drains
  • fitting weather-proof materials, joints and flashings to roofs, chimneys and walls
  • solar-powered heating systems

4. Salary

Starter: £18,000 to £22,000

Experienced: £23,000 to £35,000

These figures are a guide.

Self-employed plumbers set their own rates.

5. Working hours, patterns and environment

You'd normally work between 37 and 40 hours a week as an employee. You could work evenings, weekends and public holidays if your firm offers an emergency call-out service. Self-employed plumbers can work longer hours.

You must be able to work in confined spaces and in all weathers. You could also work on scaffolding or from ladders to repair roof guttering or leadwork.

You'd travel locally between jobs but you may have to work away from home on some contracts.

6. Career path and progression

You can develop your skills by taking further training in areas such as heating, ventilation and air-conditioning, and renewable energy technologies like solar powered heating. You can also join the Gas Safe Register or get certified with the Oil Firing Technical Association. This shows that you're legally qualified to work on gas and oil-fired appliances.

You could go on to take an HND or degree and become an engineer. This would give you career options in building services engineering, estimating and contract management.

Specialist skills training would also allow you to work in heritage conservation on historic buildings.
